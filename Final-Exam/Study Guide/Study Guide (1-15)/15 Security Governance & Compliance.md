Security governance ensures that security practices align with business goals, regulatory requirements, and organizational responsibilities.  
This section covers policy frameworks, third-party agreements, and how governance structures link policies, standards, procedures, and guidelines.  
Your exam will test your ability to identify policy types, differentiate agreements, and recognize how governance supports secure operations.

---

## 15.1 Governance Framework & Policy Hierarchy

| Concept | Expanded Definition | Nested Key Points | Workplace Example |
|---------|----------------------|--------------------|--------------------|
| ★ Security Governance | Strategic alignment of security with business objectives, regulations, and risk management. | • Ensures accountability<br>• Defines roles & responsibilities<br>• Requires compliance with laws | EisnerAmper aligning security practices with client confidentiality and compliance standards. |
| Security Policy | High-level rule defining required security behavior. | • Mandatory<br>• Organization-wide<br>• Sets expectations | Confidentiality policy for handling client PII and financial data. |
| Standard | Mandatory control supporting a policy. | • Specific requirements<br>• Enforced consistently | Password complexity standards required for Okta login. |
| Procedure | Step-by-step instructions for fulfilling a policy or standard. | • Operationalizes requirements<br>• Repeatable process | Procedure for requesting client folder access. |
| Guideline | Recommended best practice; not mandatory. | • Flexible<br>• Helps inform decisions | Suggested secure file naming format for reporting documents. |
| Policy Hierarchy | Relationship between governance elements. | • Policy → Standard → Procedure → Guideline | EisnerAmper uses procedures under its AUP and confidentiality policies. |

## 15.2 Common Security Policies

| Policy Type | Expanded Definition | Nested Key Points | Workplace Example |
|--------------|----------------------|--------------------|--------------------|
| ★ Acceptable Use Policy (AUP) | Defines appropriate and prohibited use of company systems. | • No unauthorized software<br>• No external sharing<br>• Protect sensitive data | Staff cannot send client files through personal email. |
| Incident Response Policy | Defines how incidents are reported and handled. | • Escalation paths<br>• Containment steps<br>• Communication rules | Reporting phishing emails to IT for investigation. |
| Data Classification Policy | Categorizes data by sensitivity. | • Public<br>• Internal<br>• Confidential<br>• Restricted | Client bank account sheets labeled Confidential. |
| Data Handling Policy | Dictates how each data classification must be protected. | • Storage requirements<br>• Transmission rules<br>• Access control | Confidential data must be encrypted in transit and at rest. |

## 15.3 Third-Party & Inter-Organizational Agreements

| Agreement Type | Expanded Definition | Nested Key Points | Workplace Example |
|-----------------|----------------------|--------------------|--------------------|
| ★ Third-Party Agreements | Contracts defining responsibilities between organizations. | • SLA<br>• MOU<br>• BPA<br>• NDA | Used for vendors handling client financial data. |
| SLA (Service Level Agreement) | Defines service performance expectations. | • Uptime<br>• Support response<br>• Metrics | Cloud reporting provider must meet uptime requirements. |
| MOU (Memorandum of Understanding) | Non-binding agreement describing shared goals. | • Common intentions<br>• Collaboration guidelines | Agreement with partner firms for shared reporting access. |
| BPA (Business Partnership Agreement) | Defines terms for business relationships. | • Roles<br>• Profit sharing<br>• Responsibilities | Agreement between EisnerAmper and third-party consultants. |
| NDA (Non-Disclosure Agreement) | Requires confidentiality of shared information. | • Protects sensitive data<br>• Legal obligations | Prevents disclosure of client financial statements to unauthorized parties. |
